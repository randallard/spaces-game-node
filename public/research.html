AI Research

Spaces Game is also a research platform for studying reinforcement learning in competitive strategy games — and for understanding how AI agents at different skill levels match up against real human players.

The game as a research environment

Each round of Spaces Game is a simultaneous, imperfect-information contest: both players build their boards before either one is revealed, then the boards race against each other. This makes it a rich environment for training and testing AI agents — there's no turn-order advantage, bluffing is meaningful, and the fog of war means neither side ever has the full picture.

Unlike chess or Go, where perfect play is well-studied, Spaces Game is an original ruleset where the optimal strategy is genuinely unknown. That makes it useful for studying how agents learn from scratch.

How the agents are trained

AI opponents are trained using reinforcement learning — the agent plays millions of games, receives rewards for winning, and gradually figures out better strategies without being told any rules explicitly.

1
Scripted opponents
The agent starts by playing against simple rule-based opponents with predictable strategies. This gives it a foundation to build on before anything more adaptive is introduced.
2
Self-play curriculum
The agent begins playing against saved copies of itself from earlier in training. A curriculum progressively introduces stronger opponents so the agent is always playing near the edge of its current ability.
3
Fog of war & opponent history
Agents learn under fog-of-war conditions — they only see what their opponent reveals by how far their piece gets. They also observe a history of their opponent's past boards, letting them adapt to patterns across rounds.
4
✓
Checkpoint snapshots at skill levels
Model checkpoints are saved at different points during training — early, mid, and late — giving us distinct skill levels that represent real stages of learning rather than artificial difficulty dials.

The training algorithm is Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) with action masking, implemented using Stable-Baselines3 and a custom Gymnasium environment. Full source at spaces-game-python.

Skill levels

Each level corresponds to a checkpoint from a real training run. The _plus variants use deterministic action selection (always picks the highest-probability move) while the base levels sample stochastically, making them more varied and less predictable.

What we want to learn

The core research question is: how do AI agents at different stages of training compare against human players of varying experience?

When you play against an AI opponent, your game becomes a data point in that question. We record the boards you both build and the outcome — which lets us study things like:

  • Which skill levels are genuinely challenging for new players vs. experienced ones?
  • Do humans exploit weaknesses in early-stage agents that self-play training doesn't discover?
  • Do players adapt their strategy across rounds when playing the same agent repeatedly?
  • Does the agent's fog-of-war modeling reflect what humans actually do?

Human vs. AI game data is rare in novel rulesets. Every game you play genuinely contributes to a dataset that doesn't exist anywhere else.

Common questions

Can I see the AI's code?
Yes. The training environment and inference server are open source at github.com/randallard/spaces-game-python.
Will my game data be used to train future agents?
Possibly. The current agents are pre-trained and don't update in real time, but the dataset we're building may inform future training runs. That's part of why we ask for your consent — and why "No data" is a real option, not a formality.
What if I don't want my games used?
Set your data sharing preference to "No data" in your Profile and nothing is written to the research database. You can change this at any time.

Responsible AI — by design

The initial agents were trained on a recycled Dell Optiplex with a hand-me-down GPU — no cloud datacenters, no large energy footprint. That was a deliberate choice, not a limitation we're trying to overcome.

This project is an attempt to show that meaningful AI research can be done ecologically (low energy, reused hardware), economically (accessible without institutional resources), and socially responsibly (transparent about methods and data use, designed so AI augments rather than harms the communities it touches).

We're not claiming to have solved these tensions — we're striving toward them, openly, and we think that effort is worth naming.
